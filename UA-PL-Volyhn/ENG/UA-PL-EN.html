Polish–Ukrainian Conflict 1918–1947

Historical events have divided, divide, and will continue to divide nations — but what matters is the ability to call a criminal a criminal.

Motivation and Caveats

The aim of this document is to present the Polish–Ukrainian conflict as objectively as possible, taking into account the perspectives of both sides. This is not a propaganda narrative, nor an exercise in settling historical scores.

I am Polish — and I am aware that this shapes my perception of history. I am not a historian. I do, however, try to step beyond my own narrative: I know that Lviv was founded by the Rurikid dynasty, that the Ruthenian nobility was an integral part of the Polish–Lithuanian Commonwealth's nobility, and that Ukrainian historical identity in these lands is equal to Polish identity. I write this not to diminish the Polish heritage, but to show that honest conversation requires acknowledging facts on both sides.

The document is deliberately simplified — some facts, contexts and events have been omitted to prevent it from becoming an encyclopedia. The goal is not to exhaust the subject, but to present a clear and accessible overview of those times.

No reparation — however great — can restore the dignity of a victim killed in a bestial manner. This is not a question of money or political gestures. The only thing we can do together is to preserve the memory of those ordinary people: their names, their places, their stories. To ensure they do not die a second time — this time in oblivion.

General context 💀 Atrocity Controversies: Polish Ukrainian PPR
November 1918 – June 1919 context

Polish–Ukrainian War. Ukrainians proclaimed the Western Ukrainian People's Republic (WUPR) with Lviv as its capital. Poland won the war, taking control of Eastern Galicia and Volhynia.

1918–1919 💀 atrocity PL & UA

Jewish pogroms during the Polish–Ukrainian War. Jews were victims of both sides of the conflict. Lviv pogrom (November 1918): after Polish forces took Lviv, soldiers and civilians carried out a pogrom — estimates: 52–150 killed, hundreds wounded, mass looting. The pretext was accusing Jews of neutrality or sympathy with Ukrainians. On the Ukrainian side, units of Ataman Semosenko carried out pogroms in controlled towns (Proskuriv 1919 — approx. 1,500 victims). Jews were treated as outsiders by both sides — despite having lived on these lands for centuries.

▶ Comparison — pogroms during the Russian Civil War (1918–1921) The pogroms in Western Ukraine were not an exception — they were part of a wave of anti-Jewish violence sweeping the entire post-Soviet space:

White armies (Denikin, Wrangel): systematic pogroms in Ukraine in 1919, treated by some officers as part of the fight against "Jewish Bolshevism". Estimates: 50–150 thousand Jewish victims killed by White forces.

Ukrainian warlords (Makhno, Semosenko and others): pogroms in hundreds of towns; combined victims of Ukrainian forces estimated at 30–50 thousand.

Bolsheviks: officially opposed to antisemitism, however some Red Army units also carried out pogroms — on a much smaller scale.

The total number of Jews killed in all pogroms in 1918–1921 is estimated at 50–200 thousand — making this period one of the greatest waves of anti-Jewish violence before the Holocaust.
▶ My Opinion It is worth noting that both sides — Polish and Ukrainian — formally condemned these crimes. The Second Polish Republic conducted investigations into the Lviv pogrom and brought perpetrators before courts. On the Ukrainian side, Petliura himself issued orders forbidding pogroms. It is also significant that in 1921–1939 the Second Polish Republic treated anti-Jewish actions by nationalists as criminal offences — meaning the state as an institution did not discriminate against Jews at a legal level, though the same cannot be said of public sentiment and nationalist circles.
April 1920 context

Warsaw Agreement — Piłsudski–Petliura alliance. Poland recognises the Ukrainian People's Republic (UPR) of Symon Petliura in exchange for Polish control over Eastern Galicia — the UPR formally cedes the lands east of the Zbruch. Polish-Ukrainian forces take Kyiv, but the Red Army counteroffensive reaches Warsaw (August 1920).

▶ Comparison — the same dynamic, 1939 British and French guarantees to Poland (1939): formal military commitments without real execution — Poland alone in the face of invasion. In both cases, the smaller partner in the alliance was sacrificed for a "greater purpose".
▶ My Opinion The UPR's cession of lands was a forced decision, not a voluntary one. Petliura had no territory, no army and no recognition from the great powers — the alliance with Poland was his only chance to return to Ukraine. The borders were meant to be a temporary matter.
March 1921 context

Treaty of Riga. Peace concluded without UPR participation, dividing Ukraine between Poland and Soviet Russia.

▶ Comparison — great powers deciding for others Yalta Conference (February 1945) — Roosevelt, Churchill, Stalin. Poland's borders and political system were decided without the participation of the Polish government-in-exile. Poland was shifted westward at the cost of its eastern territories.
▶ My Opinion As a Pole I understand that feeling — we ourselves feel betrayed by the decisions made at Yalta in 1945, where the great powers decided Poland's fate without the Poles. Ukrainians in 1921 must have felt exactly the same: their destiny was settled at a table at which they had no seat.
1921–1939 PL controversies

Polish policy in Western Ukraine. Systematic Polonisation of Western Ukraine through legal and demographic tools:

  • Settlement (1920–1921). The Military Settlement Act (1920) granted land to veterans of the Polish-Bolshevik War: approx. 10,000 military settler families and approx. 5,000 civilian families — a total of approximately 60,000 people settled in Western Ukraine. Land came from the parcelling of estates, often at the expense of Ukrainian peasants.
  • Language and administration (1924). Polish as the sole official language in administration and courts in Western Ukraine — Ukrainians forced to use institutions in a foreign language.
  • Land reform (1925). The Land Reform Act favoured Polish settlers in land distribution — Ukrainians as a minority had restricted access despite constituting the majority of the rural population.
  • Church and religion. Favouring of the Catholic Church (Polish) at the expense of the Greek Catholic and Orthodox Churches (Ukrainian) — including seizure of churches, restricting parish activities.
  • Civil service. Ukrainians practically excluded from senior positions in state administration and the military in Western Ukraine.

This period was characterised by deep political instability in the Second Polish Republic:

  • Assassination of Narutowicz (1922). Gabriel Narutowicz, the first president of Poland elected in part by the votes of national minorities (Ukrainians, Jews, Germans), was murdered after 5 days in office by a right-wing nationalist. It demonstrated the strength of right-wing nationalism and hostility toward policies of minority inclusion.
  • Right-wing governments 1922–1926. Successive cabinets led increasingly restrictive policies toward minorities. The culmination was the Grabski Act (1924) — drastic reduction of Ukrainian-language schools and their replacement with "utraquist" schools, effectively meaning the Polonisation of education.
  • May Coup (1926). Józef Piłsudski overthrew the democratically elected government and introduced an authoritarian system. His policies toward minorities were more pragmatic than those of the nationalist right — however, he did not abolish the discriminatory practices in Western Ukraine.
▶ Comparison — Bolsheviks toward Ukrainians (1920s) At the same time, on the other side of the border, the Bolsheviks pursued a policy of Ukrainisation (korenizatsiya, 1923–1933) — actively promoting the Ukrainian language in schools, administration, culture and media. Ukrainian became the official language of Soviet Ukraine.
▶ My Opinion Ukrainian grievances here are entirely understandable — what Poles were doing in Western Ukraine, Poles themselves had experienced under partitions as Germanisation and Russification. We knew exactly how it felt, and we did the same thing. In retrospect, Soviet tolerance was performative — a tool to draw Ukrainians into power structures, not genuine recognition of their rights.
1920–1929 UA controversies

Formation of UVO and OUN. Ukrainian Military Organization (UVO, 1920) — the first Ukrainian paramilitary organization; from it emerged the Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists (OUN, 1929). A wave of arson attacks on Polish farms and sabotage of infrastructure.

Scale of activity:

  • Sabotage campaign 1930 — approx. 2,000 acts of arson, destruction of telegraph lines and railway sabotage in Eastern Galicia
  • Political assassinations 1921–1939 — over 60 confirmed attacks and assassination attempts on Polish officials, police and activists
  • Raids on post offices and banks (funding activities) — several dozen documented operations
▶ Comparison — radicalisation of nationalism on both sides During the same period, analogous extreme movements were forming on the Polish side:

Camp of Great Poland (OWP, 1926) — Roman Dmowski, integral nationalism, antisemitism, vision of an ethnically homogeneous Poland.

National Radical Camp (ONR, 1934) — more radical, modelled on Italian and German fascism. Attacks on Jews, the slogan "Poland for Poles".

Context: the 1920s and 30s were a period of radicalisation across all of Europe — NSDAP, Italian fascists, Spanish Falange. Both OUN and ONR were children of the same era, not anomalies.
▶ My Opinion Resistance to subjugation is natural and justified. It is worth noting, however, that OUN's activities in this period were directed primarily at functionaries — officials, police, politicians — not at the civilian population.
1930 PL controversies

Pacification of Eastern Lesser Poland (September–November 1930). The authorities' response to the OUN sabotage campaign. Reprisals were directed collectively, without distinguishing the guilty.

  • Approx. 500–800 villages covered by the pacification operation
  • Approx. 500–800 homes and farm buildings destroyed or burned
  • Approx. 1,600–2,000 people arrested and interrogated, many beaten
  • Over 30 Prosvita reading rooms (Ukrainian cultural centres) vandalised or closed
  • Confiscation of livestock: horses, cattle, grain
  • Deaths: several to a dozen or so (Polish and Ukrainian sources differ significantly)
▶ My Opinion The Pacification was a political move that revealed the weakness of the Second Polish Republic more than its strength. Resorting to collective reprisals without a legal basis, directed at entire villages rather than specific perpetrators, was an admission of inability to solve the problem through the rule of law.
1931–1934 UA controversies

OUN assassinations. A series of political murders targeting representatives of the Polish state:

  • 1931 — Tadeusz Hołówko (MP, advocate of Polish–Ukrainian dialogue) — shot in Truskavets. Paradoxically, OUN eliminated one of the few Poles open to compromise.
  • 1934 — Bronisław Pieracki (Minister of the Interior) — shot in Warsaw. The assassination was the direct impulse for the creation of the Bereza Kartuska detention camp.
▶ My Opinion The murder of Hołówko made no sense to me. He was one of the very few high-ranking Polish politicians genuinely working toward Polish–Ukrainian reconciliation. OUN eliminated one of their potential allies on the Polish side.
1934–1939 PL controversies

Bereza Kartuska. The only internment camp of the Second Polish Republic, established after the Pieracki assassination. Nationalists (OUN, ONR), communists and political opponents were interned without trial. A total of approx. 3–3.5 thousand prisoners; mortality: 13–30 persons (~0.5%).

1932–1933 💀 atrocity (USSR)

Holodomor. A famine deliberately caused by Soviet authorities, directed against the Ukrainian countryside. Victims: approx. 3.5–7 million Ukrainians.

17 September 1939 – 1941 context

First Soviet occupation. The USSR enters Western Ukraine. Mass deportations of the Polish population into the USSR (approx. 320–400 thousand people). Some Ukrainians welcomed the Soviets with relief — which entrenched memories of mutual grievances on both sides. Just like the Polish elite, the Ukrainian elite was systematically liquidated by the NKVD — arrests, executions and deportations affected Ukrainian activists, teachers, clergy and intelligentsia.

▶ My Opinion The satisfaction of some Ukrainians with the Soviet entry likely stemmed from their experience of the Polish system — after years of Polonisation and discrimination, any change might have seemed better. The Soviets brought not freedom — but a different kind of subjugation.
1941 💀 UA atrocity

OUN and the Germans — pogroms. Bandera proclaimed Ukrainian independence in Lviv on 30 June 1941; the Germans rejected it and arrested him. Part of OUN nonetheless participated in Jewish pogroms (Lviv 1941) and served in auxiliary police. The Ukrainian 14th Waffen-SS Division "Galicia" fought on the German side.

▶ Comparison — other "independences" under German patronage Slovakia (from March 1939): the government of Jozef Tiso — Slovakia deported its own Jews to extermination camps. Assessment: a puppet state, not sovereign.

Croatia — NDH (from April 1941): the Ustasha in power — territory divided between German and Italian spheres of influence. Assessment: a puppet state, not sovereign.
February–March 1943 💀 UA atrocity

First mass attacks by UPA. Dozens of Polish villages in Volhynia burned, thousands of victims.

11 July 1943 — Bloody Sunday 💀 UA atrocity

Simultaneous attacks on approx. 167 localities during Sunday masses. Estimates: 5–8 thousand victims in one day.

▶ My Opinion I consider the UPA campaign to have been systematic and planned, not spontaneous. The attacks covered many villages simultaneously across a vast area. They lasted for months. If they had arisen purely from accumulated hatred and emotion, why did they not erupt earlier — under Polish rule, when Ukrainian grievances were fresh? The campaign launched at a moment when the Second Polish Republic no longer existed, Germany and the Soviets were bleeding each other, and Volhynia was a land without any real sovereign. This was a political and military calculation, not an eruption of rage. The absence of a document ordering extermination does not mean the absence of a decision.
Summer–Autumn 1943 💀 UA atrocity

Systematic "cleansing" of Volhynia — UPA attacks successive villages and towns. Survivors flee to cities or form self-defence units.

Autumn 1943 – 1944 💀 PL atrocity

Polish reprisal operations. The Home Army (AK) and Peasant Battalions (BCh) attack Ukrainian villages suspected of collaboration with UPA. The scale is incomparable to UPA's actions, but the attacks were also directed at civilians.

March 1944 — Sahryń 💀 PL atrocity

A reprisal operation by the AK and BCh against the village of Sahryń (inhabited by Ukrainians). Victims: approx. 200–1,000 (historians' estimates vary significantly). A crime against civilians — unacceptable regardless of context.

▶ My Opinion These crimes cannot be attributed to the entire AK formation. The AK had a formal rule forbidding attacks on civilians — which means Sahryń was a violation of the Underground State's own law, not its implementation.
1943–1944 — Eastern Galicia 💀 UA atrocity

UPA extends operations to Eastern Galicia — continuation of murders amid growing resistance from Polish self-defence units.

▶ My Opinion The fighting against combatants cost UPA approx. 15,000 casualties on the Soviet side, approx. 5,000 on the Polish side, and fewer than 1,000 on the German side. This gives pause for thought — attacks on the civilian population were clearly of greater priority to UPA commanders than actual combat against the occupier.
Balance 1943–1945 context

Approx. 60–100 thousand Poles killed by UPA. Approx. 10–15 thousand Ukrainians killed in Polish reprisal operations. The vast majority of victims on both sides were civilians.

Context of attacks: UPA deliberately chose Sundays and religious holidays.

UPA methods: burning alive, drowning in wells and rivers, burying alive, impalement, nailing heads, disembowelling pregnant women, dismemberment by horses, axes, saws, pitchforks.

AK/BCh methods (reprisal operations): burning Ukrainian villages, shootings, beatings. Although the scale and systematicity were incomparable to UPA's actions, the attacks also struck civilians, including women and children.

▶ Comparison — Ustasha and Serbs (1941–1945) The Croatian fascist movement Ustasha carried out systematic extermination of Serbs, Jews and Roma in the Independent State of Croatia (NDH) in 1941 — on the same territory, at the same time as UPA's crimes.

Scale: 300–800 thousand Serbian victims.

Methods: mass shootings, throat-cutting with special knives (srbosjek), burning alive in Orthodox churches, Jasenovac extermination camp (approx. 80–100 thousand victims).

Context: like UPA, the Ustasha were nationalists seeking an ethnically pure territory under the cover of German occupation.

Croatia 2000–2013 — official rejection of the Ustasha legacy: NDH was unambiguously recognised as a collaborationist and criminal regime. The state supported memory of Jasenovac victims. In the EU accession process, Croatia was subject to pressure regarding minority rights and cooperation with ICTY.

The crimes in Volhynia were not an anomaly — they were part of a broader European phenomenon of nationalist extermination during the Second World War.
1944–1946 context

Forced population transfer. Approx. 1.5 million Poles resettled from Western Ukraine to Poland (so-called repatriation — in reality, expulsion). Approx. 500 thousand Ukrainians resettled from Poland to the USSR.

1945–1947 — UPA vs PPR PPR controversies

UPA still active. After the end of the war, UPA continued partisan warfare against PPR and NKVD units in south-eastern Poland. These activities were used by the PPR authorities as justification for Operation Vistula.

1947 — Operation Vistula PPR controversies

Approx. 150,000 Ukrainians, Lemkos and Boyko were forcibly resettled from south-eastern Poland to the western territories — dispersed, with the aim of denationalisation. Official justification: pacification of UPA's rear base. In practice: collective punishment of the entire civilian population.

▶ My Opinion It is worth remembering that the PPR is not regarded by most Poles as the legal successor to the Second Polish Republic. The PPR was a creation imposed by a foreign power, just like the communist governments in Czechoslovakia, Hungary or Romania.

My Opinion

On the Second Polish Republic

The Second Polish Republic was the Poland that was possible — not ideal. A state rebuilt after 123 years of partition, stitched together from three different legal and administrative systems, surrounded from the outset by hostile neighbours. It discriminated against minorities, turned authoritarian after 1926, and never escaped poverty. And yet — against the backdrop of the totalitarianisms that destroyed it — it remained a space in which an independent press, courts and political parties functioned. This is not a defence of its errors. This is context, without which any assessment is dishonest.

On Ukrainians under two occupations

Ukrainians found themselves in a tragic position — between a repressive Polish administration and Soviet genocide. The aspirations for their own state were entirely justified. Resorting to collaboration with the Germans was a political error and a moral catastrophe.

The UPA's actions were not a spontaneous act of revenge for years of oppression — they were a planned extermination operation directed against civilians. The scale and methods cannot be justified by any context.

On heroes and criminals

It is possible to honour one's own nation's heroes without falsifying history. The Lviv Eaglets and the soldiers of the WUPR fought for the same city on opposite sides — neither side's heroism is diminished by this. All the more so is there no need to seek heroes among perpetrators of crimes against civilians.

One may argue that Volhynia was a response to years of Polish occupation. One may argue that the AK operations were a response to the murders of Poles. Yet in both cases the same question arises: what did that child do? If death was the punishment for children — then guilt was not the criterion. People were dehumanised and murdered to vent the animal instincts that someone called justice.

What Next?

Rebuilding relations after such experiences is one of the most difficult tasks two nations can face. It is not about forgetting — that would be impossible and dishonest toward the victims. It is about something harder: the ability to look at another human being across a wall of memories, grievances and mutual wrongs. This requires work, time, sacrifice — and above all, the will on both sides. There are no shortcuts.

The crimes have been committed. We cannot undo them, we should not try to outbid each other with them. What matters most is the commemoration of ordinary people — killed simply for being in that place and speaking a different language. Do we truly want hatred to be what we leave behind?

Questions to begin with — Ukrainian side

  • Does Ukrainian national identity truly need UPA as its symbol? Ivan Mazepa, Taras Shevchenko, Mykhailo Hrushevsky — they fought for Ukraine without murdering their neighbours' children. Western Europe has found the line between honouring resistance and glorifying war criminals — can Ukraine do the same?
  • Could shared memorial sites and dignified exhumations of Volhynia's victims be a first concrete step — not a political gesture, but a human minimum for the families who still do not know where their loved ones lie?
  • Is Ukrainian historical narrative able to acknowledge that the victims of Volhynia were not "Polish oppressors" — but ordinary people, peasants, children, priests, who had nothing to do with the decisions of Warsaw politicians?
  • Does Ukraine need UPA to have something to be proud of? Kyivan Rus', the Cossacks, the Holodomor, the struggle for independence in 1918–1920 — Ukrainian history is rich and dramatic. Why should the symbol of strength and resistance be precisely the one who murdered his neighbours' children?

Questions to begin with — Polish side

  • Can we speak honestly about the Second Polish Republic — acknowledging that it discriminated against minorities and was authoritarian — without feeling that we are betraying the memory of the generation that built it?
  • Can the reprisal operations of the AK and BCh against Ukrainian civilians be called by their name — without feeling that we are relativising the crimes of Volhynia?
  • Are we able to acknowledge that some of those regarded as great founding fathers of the nation made at the very least controversial decisions? Piłsudski overthrew a democratically elected government and ruled autocratically. Dmowski built his vision of Poland on antisemitism and ethnic purity. The pacification policy in Western Ukraine was carried out by men we glorify today. Acknowledging this is not betrayal — it is maturity.
  • Are we able to state plainly that slogans such as "Poland for Poles" are nationalist slogans — and that they should be condemned, just as we condemn the nationalism of other nations? Polish identity does not need to be defined through exclusion.

The list of questions for both sides is incomplete — deliberately so. I believe that a person coming from Ukrainian education and the Ukrainian community would raise far more questions directed at their own side, just as I could extend this list further on our Polish side. What is written here is a possible entry point — not a conclusion.
