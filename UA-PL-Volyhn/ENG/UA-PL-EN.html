Polish-Ukrainian Conflict 1918-1947

Historical events have divided, divide, and will continue to divide nations — but what matters is the ability to call a criminal a criminal.

Motivation and Caveats

The aim of this document is to present the Polish-Ukrainian conflict as objectively as possible, taking into account the perspectives of both sides. This is not a propaganda narrative, nor an exercise in settling historical scores.

I am Polish — and I am aware that this shapes my perception of history. I am not a historian. I do, however, try to step beyond my own narrative.

The document is deliberately simplified — some facts, contexts and events have been omitted to prevent it from becoming an encyclopedia. The goal is not to exhaust the subject, but to present a clear and accessible overview of those times.

The document deliberately contains no footnotes or source citations. Including specific sources would open another level of dispute — about which evidence is reliable, which is biased, and which is admissible at all. Polish and Ukrainian historiographies differ not only in their assessments, but often in the very selection of facts considered certain. Rather than adjudicating that dispute, I have chosen to present a general picture — and to leave the reader to decide where to look further.

No reparation — however great — can restore the dignity of a victim killed in a bestial manner. This is not a question of money or political gestures. The only thing we can do together is to preserve the memory of those ordinary people: their names, their places, their stories. To ensure they do not die a second time — this time in oblivion.

1917-1921 context
▶ Russian Civil War

Russian Civil War (1917-1921) - conflict following the Russian Revolution between the Bolsheviks ("Reds") and their opponents ("Whites"), as well as national movements including the UPR.

November 1918 - June 1919 context
▶ Polish-(Western)Ukrainian War

Polish-(Western)Ukrainian War.

After the collapse of Austria-Hungary, proclamation of the Western Ukrainian People's Republic (WUPR) with Lviv as its capital.

Uprising of Lviv's Polish population - the Polish population of Lviv, constituting a majority in the city, rose up arms against the WUPR's seizure of power; street fighting lasted from November 1918.

Aid from the Second Polish Republic to Poles in the fighting against WUPR.

End of fighting (17 July 1919) - WUPR forces crossed the Zbruch river, retreating to the territory of Petliura's UPR; formal end of hostilities between Poland and the WUPR.

1918-1919 💀 atrocity PL & UA
▶ Jewish pogroms during the Polish-Ukrainian War

Jewish pogroms during the Polish-Ukrainian War. Jews were victims of both sides of the conflict — despite having lived on these lands for centuries.

Lviv pogrom (November 1918): after Polish forces took Lviv, soldiers and civilians carried out a pogrom — estimates: 52-150 killed, hundreds wounded, mass looting. The pretext was accusing Jews of neutrality or sympathy with Ukrainians.

Proskuriv (1919): units of Ataman Semosenko carried out pogroms in controlled towns — approx. 1,500 victims on the Ukrainian side.

▶ Comparison — pogroms during the Russian Civil War (1918-1921) The pogroms in Western Ukraine were not an exception — they were part of a wave of anti-Jewish violence sweeping the entire post-Soviet space:

White armies (Denikin, Wrangel): systematic pogroms in Ukraine in 1919, treated by some officers as part of the fight against "Jewish Bolshevism". Estimates: 50-150 thousand Jewish victims killed by White forces.

Ukrainian warlords (Makhno, Semosenko and others): pogroms in hundreds of towns; combined victims of Ukrainian forces estimated at 30-50 thousand.

Bolsheviks: officially opposed to antisemitism, however some Red Army units also carried out pogroms — on a much smaller scale.

The total number of Jews killed in all pogroms in 1918-1921 is estimated at 50-200 thousand — making this period one of the greatest waves of anti-Jewish violence before the Holocaust.
▶ My Opinion It is worth noting that both sides — Polish and Ukrainian — formally condemned these crimes. The Second Polish Republic conducted investigations into the Lviv pogrom and brought perpetrators before courts; verdicts were reached. On the Ukrainian side, Petliura himself issued orders forbidding pogroms, and some of his officers actively tried to suppress them — though without success against unruly units. It is also significant that in 1921-1939 the Second Polish Republic treated anti-Jewish actions by nationalists (beatings, boycotts, riots) as criminal offences — meaning the state as an institution did not discriminate against Jews at a legal level, though the same cannot be said of public sentiment and nationalist circles.
April 1920 context
▶ Warsaw Agreement — Piłsudski-Petliura alliance

Warsaw Agreement — Piłsudski-Petliura alliance. Poland recognises the Ukrainian People's Republic (UPR) of Symon Petliura and commits to military support in the fight for Ukrainian independence — in exchange the UPR formally cedes Eastern Galicia to Poland.

▶ Comparison — the same dynamic, 1939 British and French guarantees to Poland (1939): formal military commitments without real execution — Poland alone in the face of invasion. In both cases, the smaller partner in the alliance was sacrificed for a "greater purpose".
▶ My Opinion The UPR's cession of lands was a forced decision, not a voluntary one. Petliura had no territory, no army and no recognition from the great powers — the alliance with Poland was his only chance to return to Ukraine. The borders were meant to be a temporary matter.
1920 context
▶ Attempt to establish the UPR

Attempt to establish the UPR. Joint Polish-Ukrainian forces conducted a military campaign against the Bolsheviks:

  • Kyiv Offensive (1920) - offensive by the Second Polish Republic forces and allied UPR (Ukrainian People's Republic) units against the Bolsheviks; aimed at weakening Soviet Russia and supporting an independent Ukraine.
  • Battle of Warsaw (13-25 August 1920) - victory of the Second Polish Republic forces over the Red Army, which halted the Bolshevik offensive toward Western Europe.
  • Defense of Zamosc (29-31 August 1920) - defense of the city by Second Polish Republic forces against Budyonny's 1st Cavalry Army; halted Bolshevik operations in the south and helped consolidate Poland's victory in the 1920 war.

Polish-Ukrainian side:

  • Poles — approx. 80–90% (historians' estimates) — the backbone of the Second Polish Republic's armed forces
  • Ukrainians — primarily soldiers of the Ukrainian People's Republic Army under Symon Petliura; at the start of the Kyiv Offensive numbering approx. 15,000–20,000 (at its peak approx. 40,000)
  • Belarusians, Jews, Lithuanians and other citizens of the Second Polish Republic

Red Army:

  • Russians — approx. 60–70% — the largest group
  • Ukrainians — approx. 15–20%
  • Belarusians, Latvians, Tatars, Jews and others
1919-1921 💀🪖 war crime PL & USSR
▶ Prisoner of war camps — Poland and Soviet Russia

Prisoner of war camps — Poland and Soviet Russia. Both sides of the Polish-Bolshevik War held tens of thousands of prisoners in conditions that led to mass mortality.

Polish camps (Soviet prisoners):

  • Prisoners: approx. 60,000–80,000
  • Deaths: approx. 14,000–16,000
  • Mortality: approx. 15–20%
  • Causes of death: epidemics (typhus, dysentery, influenza), hunger, cold, exhaustion
  • Reason for internment: prisoners following combat with the Red Army

Soviet camps (Polish prisoners):

  • Prisoners: approx. 40,000–60,000
  • Deaths: approx. 18,000–20,000
  • Mortality: approx. 20–30%
  • Causes of death: epidemics (typhus, cholera, dysentery), hunger, malnutrition, lack of medical care
  • Reason for internment: prisoners from the Polish-Bolshevik War; chaos and lack of an efficient exchange system

In both cases the main cause of death was living conditions, not deliberate executions. The scale of losses resulted from wartime chaos, epidemics and lack of infrastructure — not from a policy of extermination — which distinguishes these camps from the later Soviet Gulag or Nazi camps.

March 1921 PL controversies
▶ Treaty of Riga

Treaty of Riga. Peace concluded without UPR participation, dividing Ukraine between Poland and Soviet Russia. For Ukrainians this date marked the formal beginning of two parallel occupations: Polish in the west (Eastern Galicia, Volhynia) and Soviet in the east.

▶ Comparison - Seizure of Vilnius by the Second Polish Republic (1920-1922) Gen. Lucjan Żeligowski staged a feigned "mutiny" and, on Piłsudski's orders, seized Vilnius and its surrounding territory, creating the puppet statelet of "Central Lithuania". In 1922 the area was formally incorporated into Poland following elections boycotted by Lithuanians and Jews. Lithuania treated this as an illegal occupation throughout the interwar period and maintained no diplomatic relations with Poland.
▶ Opinion As a Pole I understand that feeling — we ourselves feel betrayed by the decisions made at Yalta in 1945, where the great powers decided Poland's fate without the Poles. Ukrainians in 1921 must have felt exactly the same: their destiny was settled at a table at which they had no seat.
▶ Additional Opinion The Second Polish Republic was also demonstrating its intention to expand its influence despite the opposition of local nationalities.
1920-1924 PL controversies
▶ Internment camps for Ukrainian armed formations (UPR and UHA)

Internment camps for Ukrainian armed formations (UPR and UHA). Following the collapse of the Polish-Ukrainian alliance and the end of hostilities, Poland interned the disarmed Ukrainian formations: UPR units (Petliura's) and UHA (the Ukrainian Halychian Army from Eastern Galicia).

  • Internees: approx. 20,000–25,000
  • Deaths: 500–1,000
  • Mortality: approx. 1–5%
  • Causes of death: infectious diseases, malnutrition, overcrowding, camp conditions
  • Reason for internment: disarmed Ukrainian formations following the collapse of the alliance and the end of hostilities

1922–1924 — gradual closure of camps and release of internees. Some Ukrainians emigrated to the West, some returned to Soviet Ukraine, some remained in Poland.

Piłsudski's visit to the camp (15 May 1921). This episode is well documented. On 15 May 1921 Józef Piłsudski visited a camp of interned UPR soldiers (sources give Kalisz/Szczypiorno, depending on the account) and is said to have told them: "I apologise to you, gentlemen. I apologise to you deeply. It was not supposed to be this way."

▶ Opinion Mortality was many times lower than in the prisoner of war camps (1919–1921) and resulted mainly from living conditions, not deliberate policy. Nonetheless, the internment of disarmed allies — without a clear legal basis and without trial — constitutes a politically and morally controversial precedent.
1919-1939 context
▶ Mixed marriages on the Borderlands

Mixed marriages on the Borderlands. In mixed Polish-Ukrainian families, an informal custom of inheriting nationality by gender was practiced: daughters took the mother's nationality, sons took the father's nationality. In the context of the Volhynian massacres, this meant that a Ukrainian wife or her relatives often knew about the planned attack — and it was through this family bond that they warned or sheltered their Polish kin.

1921-1939 PL controversies
▶ Polish policy in Western Ukraine

Polish policy in Western Ukraine. Systematic Polonisation of Western Ukraine through legal and demographic tools:

  • Settlement (1920-1921). The Military Settlement Act (1920) granted land to veterans of the Polish-Bolshevik War: approx. 10,000 military settler families and approx. 5,000 civilian families — a total of approximately 60,000 people settled in Western Ukraine. Land came from the parcelling of estates, often at the expense of Ukrainian peasants.
  • Language and administration (1924). Polish as the sole official language in administration and courts in Western Ukraine — Ukrainians forced to use institutions in a foreign language.
  • Education (1924). Polish as the sole language of instruction in many schools in Western Ukraine — restriction of Ukrainian-language schooling and forcing Ukrainians to receive education in Polish.
  • Land reform (1925). The Land Reform Act favoured Polish settlers in land distribution — Ukrainians as a minority had restricted access despite constituting the majority of the rural population.
  • Church and religion. Favouring of the Catholic Church (Polish) at the expense of the Greek Catholic and Orthodox Churches (Ukrainian) — including seizure of churches, restricting parish activities.
  • Civil service. Ukrainians practically excluded from senior positions in state administration and the military in Western Ukraine.

This period was characterised by deep political instability in the Second Polish Republic:

  • Assassination of Narutowicz (1922). Gabriel Narutowicz, the first president of Poland elected in part by the votes of national minorities (Ukrainians, Jews, Germans), was murdered after 5 days in office by a right-wing nationalist. It demonstrated the strength of right-wing nationalism and hostility toward policies of minority inclusion.
  • Right-wing governments 1922-1926. Successive cabinets led increasingly restrictive policies toward minorities — restriction of autonomy, military settlement in Western Ukraine as a deliberate demographic tool. The culmination was the Grabski Act (1924) — drastic reduction of Ukrainian-language schools and their replacement with "utraquist" (Polish-Ukrainian) schools, effectively meaning the Polonisation of education.
  • May Coup (1926). Józef Piłsudski overthrew the democratically elected government and introduced an authoritarian system. Paradoxically, his policies toward minorities were more pragmatic than those of the nationalist right — however, he did not abolish the discriminatory practices in Western Ukraine.
▶ Comparison — Bolsheviks toward Ukrainians (1920s) At the same time, on the other side of the border, the Bolsheviks pursued a policy of Ukrainisation (korenizatsiya, 1923-1933) — actively promoting the Ukrainian language in schools, administration, culture and media. Ukrainian became the official language of Soviet Ukraine. Ukrainian universities, theatres and a press were established.
▶ My Opinion Ukrainian grievances here are entirely understandable — what Poles were doing in Western Ukraine, Poles themselves had experienced under partitions as Germanisation and Russification. We knew exactly how it felt, and we did the same thing. For a Ukrainian living under Polish Polonisation, the Soviet model may have appeared paradoxically more attractive — at least until Stalin reversed that policy with the Holodomor and the liquidation of the Ukrainian intelligentsia. In retrospect, Soviet tolerance was performative — a tool to draw Ukrainians into power structures, not genuine recognition of their rights.
1920-1929 UA controversies
▶ Formation of UVO and OUN

Formation of UVO and OUN. Ukrainian Military Organization (UVO, 1920) — the first Ukrainian paramilitary organization; from it emerged the Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists (OUN, 1929). A wave of arson attacks on Polish farms and sabotage of infrastructure.

Scale of activity:

  • Sabotage campaign 1930 — approx. 2,000 acts of arson, destruction of telegraph lines and railway sabotage in Eastern Galicia; directly preceded by this campaign, the Pacification
  • Political assassinations 1921-1939 — over 60 confirmed attacks and assassination attempts on Polish officials, police and activists
  • Raids on post offices and banks (funding activities) — several dozen documented operations
▶ Comparison — radicalisation of nationalism on both sides During the same period, analogous extreme movements were forming on the Polish side:

Camp of Great Poland (OWP, 1926) — Roman Dmowski, integral nationalism, antisemitism, vision of an ethnically homogeneous Poland.

National Radical Camp (ONR, 1934) — more radical, modelled on Italian and German fascism. Attacks on Jews, student militias, the slogan "Poland for Poles". Banned by the Second Polish Republic's government after a few months of activity; following the ban it split into ONR-ABC and Falanga.

Context: the 1920s and 30s were a period of radicalisation across all of Europe — NSDAP, Italian fascists, Spanish Falange. Both OUN and ONR were children of the same era, not anomalies.
▶ My Opinion Resistance to subjugation is natural and justified. A nation deprived of other political instruments reaches for what remains — and what we today call terrorist acts were at the time the only available form of resistance for the weaker side. It is worth noting, however, that OUN's activities in this period were directed primarily at functionaries — officials, police, politicians — not at the civilian population.
1922 UA controversies
▶ Sydir Tverdokhlib — murdered by UVO

Sydir Tverdokhlib — Ukrainian poet, journalist and public activist, murdered by UVO.

  • He studied at Lviv University and worked for the development of Ukrainian culture and national life.
  • He was associated with the Ukrainian Peasant (Agrarian) Party and supported Ukrainian participation in the Polish political system.
  • In 1922 he stood as a candidate in elections to the Polish Sejm despite the boycott declared by some Ukrainian groups.
  • 15 October 1922 he was shot by members of UVO.
1930 PL controversies
▶ Pacification of Eastern Lesser Poland

Pacification of Eastern Lesser Poland (September-November 1930). The authorities' response to the OUN sabotage campaign. Reprisals were directed collectively, without distinguishing the guilty.

  • Approx. 500-800 villages covered by the pacification operation
  • Approx. 500-800 homes and farm buildings destroyed or burned
  • Approx. 1,600-2,000 people arrested and interrogated, many beaten
  • Over 30 Prosvita reading rooms (Ukrainian cultural centres) vandalised or closed
  • Confiscation of livestock: horses, cattle, grain
  • Deaths: several to a dozen or so (Polish and Ukrainian sources differ significantly)
▶ Comparison — Holodomor (USSR, 1932-1933) Two years after the Pacification, on the other side of the border, Soviet authorities deliberately caused a famine directed against the Ukrainian countryside. Victims: approx. 3.5-7 million Ukrainians.

The comparison is not meant to relativise the Pacification — but to show the geopolitical context in which Ukrainians lived that decade: repression from the Polish side, genocide from the Soviet side. The scale is incomparable, but in both cases the victim was the same Ukrainian countryside.
▶ My Opinion The Pacification was a political move that revealed the weakness of the Second Polish Republic more than its strength. Resorting to collective reprisals without a legal basis, directed at entire villages rather than specific perpetrators, was an admission of inability to solve the problem through the rule of law. One thing that can be noted on the positive side: village mayors and district heads were informed in advance by ultimatum, giving some warning. That is, however, insufficient to call this a lawful operation.
1931-1934 UA controversies
▶ OUN assassinations — Hołówko and Pieracki

OUN assassinations. A series of political murders targeting representatives of the Polish state:

  • 1931 — Tadeusz Hołówko (MP, advocate of Polish-Ukrainian dialogue) — shot in Truskavets. Paradoxically, OUN eliminated one of the few Poles open to compromise.
  • 1934 — Bronisław Pieracki (Minister of the Interior) — shot in Warsaw. Perpetrator: Hryhoriy Matseiko on OUN-B's orders. Pieracki had been proclaimed by OUN as the main person responsible for the Pacification of Eastern Lesser Poland (1930). The assassination was the direct impulse for the creation of the Bereza Kartuska detention camp.
1934-1939 PL controversies
▶ Bereza Kartuska

Bereza Kartuska. The only internment camp of the Second Polish Republic, established after the Pieracki assassination. Nationalists (OUN, ONR), communists and political opponents were interned without trial.

Ethnic composition:

  • Ukrainians — approx. 50–70% — mostly OUN activists and related nationalist organisations; dominant throughout the camp's existence
  • Poles — approx. 20–40% — two main subgroups: the far right (ONR and sympathisers) and communists and left-wing activists; their share grew particularly toward the end of the 1930s
  • Jews — approx. 5–10% — mainly communist or opposition activists; present in proportion to their share in left-wing movements
  • Other nationalities (Lithuanians, Belarusians, Russians and others) — a few % — small groups, usually connected with the communist movement or activities in the Borderlands

▶ My Opinion Bereza is further evidence of the state's weakness in the face of nationalist movements. Instead of effective integration policies or law enforcement through courts, the Second Polish Republic resorted to internment without trial — a method that effectively admitted the legal system was insufficient. Compared to the Gulag the scale is incomparable, but the principle is the same: isolation of political opponents outside the framework of law.
1938-1939 context
▶ Carpathian Sich

After the Munich Agreement and the weakening of Czechoslovakia (1938), Carpathian Ruthenia gained autonomy, and Ukrainian activists formed the Carpathian Sich — a paramilitary organisation defending the region and supporting the idea of an independent Ukraine.

On 15 March 1939, following the declaration of independence of Carpatho-Ukraine, Sich units fought unsuccessfully against the Hungarian invasion. After the defeat the organisation ceased to exist.

During the Second World War its former members were killed, arrested or emigrated; some later served in OUN and UPA or other military formations.

22 March 1939 💀🪖 war crime PL/Hungary
▶ Veretsky Pass — Carpathian Sich

Veretsky Pass. After the fall of Carpathian Ukraine, soldiers of the Carpathian Sich were fleeing Hungarian forces toward the Polish border. KOP (Border Protection Corps) units had orders to disarm and intern them.

At the Veretsky Pass approximately 43 Sich soldiers were killed. No casualties were recorded on the Polish side. The number of persons disarmed, let through or handed to Hungarian authorities remains unknown.

If those killed had previously been disarmed or detained, their deaths would have been contrary to the orders issued.

The circumstances are not clear-cut. There is no conclusive evidence regarding the sequence of events — the victims may have been killed in combat engagements rather than by execution. Simultaneously, Hungarian forces were actively conducting operations against retreating Carpathian Sich units, meaning some casualties may have occurred in mixed combat episodes involving several forces.

▶ Opinion If those killed had previously been disarmed and detained — it is a crime, regardless of context. If they died in combat — it is a war tragedy. The problem is that without conclusive evidence and given the actions of many sides simultaneously (KOP, Hungarians, the chaos of retreat) it is impossible to determine with certainty what exactly happened. The fact that no one was tried does not decide guilt — but neither does it exclude it.
September 1939 💀 UA atrocity
▶ OUN and allied Ukrainian militias — anti-Polish actions

OUN and allied Ukrainian militias — anti-Polish actions. Following the Red Army's entry into the Eastern Borderlands, OUN units and local Ukrainian militias exploited the chaos of the invasion to attack the Polish civilian population, soldiers and officials in Eastern Galicia and Volhynia.

  • Polish civilian victims: approx. 2–3 thousand people (mainly in Eastern Galicia and Volhynia).
  • Polish armed victims (soldiers, police, officials): several hundred people (some sources give approx. several hundred killed in OUN actions).

Brzeżany and surroundings (September 1939) — units of Ukrainian nationalists linked to OUN attacked Polish estates and localities; in the Brzeżany and Podhajce area, several hundred Poles were killed (mostly civilians).

17 September 1939 - 1941 context
▶ First Soviet occupation

First Soviet occupation. The USSR enters Western Ukraine. Mass deportations of the Polish population into the USSR (approx. 320-400 thousand people). Some Ukrainians welcomed the Soviets with relief — which entrenched memories of mutual grievances on both sides. Just like the Polish elite, the Ukrainian elite was systematically liquidated by the NKVD — arrests, executions and deportations affected Ukrainian activists, teachers, clergy and intelligentsia.

The four main waves of deportation were roughly as follows:

  • 10 February 1940 - approx. 140,000 people, mainly military settlers and foresters with their families.
  • 13 April 1940 - approx. 60,000-61,000 people, primarily families of officers, officials and previously arrested persons.
  • June-July 1940 - approx. 80,000 people, including many refugees from German-occupied Poland, among them many Jews.
  • May-June 1941 - approx. 40,000-45,000 people, classified by the Soviet authorities as an "anti-Soviet element".
▶ Comparison - Second Polish Republic and the seizure of Zaolzie (October 1938) A year before the Soviet invasion of Poland, the Second Polish Republic itself exploited the Czechoslovak crisis triggered by the Munich Agreement to seize Zaolzie (Cieszyn Silesia) by force — a Czechoslovak territory largely inhabited by Poles but part of a sovereign state.

Poland issued an ultimatum and entered on 2 October 1938, a few days after Germany occupied the Sudetenland. In the eyes of the Western powers and Czechoslovakia, it was an opportunistic move — exploiting someone else's tragedy. Winston Churchill called Poland "the hyena of Europe".

The comparison is not meant to equate the scale — Soviet occupation was incomparably more brutal. It does, however, show that the Second Polish Republic itself applied the logic of force against a weaker neighbour when circumstances allowed.
1939-1941 context
▶ Soviet propaganda and infiltration

Political propaganda. The Soviet authorities presented their invasion as the "liberation" of Ukrainians and Belarusians from Polish rule. Elections were organised to legitimise the annexation, but historians widely regard them as tightly controlled rather than free.

The Soviet press emphasised:

  • class struggle,
  • an anti-Polish message targeting "Polish landlords",
  • anti-capitalism,
  • unification with Soviet Ukraine.

NKVD infiltration.

  • infiltrated former Polish institutions,
  • recruited informants,
  • arrested Polish officials, military officers, teachers, clergy and nationalist activists,
  • monitored Ukrainian political organisations.

Many members of the Polish underground were identified thanks to informants or covert operational activities.

June-July 1941 💀🪖 war crime (USSR)
▶ NKVD murders in prisons of Western Ukraine and the Borderlands

NKVD murders in prisons of Western Ukraine and the Borderlands. When the Wehrmacht attacked the USSR (22 June 1941) and the front moved rapidly eastward, the NKVD carried out mass murders of prisoners held in Soviet prisons in Western Ukraine, Eastern Galicia and the Borderlands — to prevent them from being liberated by the Germans. Estimates: approx. 20,000-30,000 victims within a matter of days.

  • Lviv: prisons on Łąckiego and Zamarstynowska Streets — several thousand killed, bodies discovered by advancing Germans and civilians.
  • Zolochiv, Sambir, Stanyslaviv, Lutsk, Dubno — analogous murders in local prisons.
  • The NKVD did not select victims by nationality, but by category of "enemy". Among those imprisoned and murdered were:
    • state officials and functionaries,
    • military officers and persons associated with the military,
    • members of the underground, including the Service for Poland's Victory and the Union of Armed Struggle,
    • activists of the Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists (OUN),
    • members of Ukrainian political organisations,
    • persons suspected of anti-Soviet views,
    • clergy,
    • the intelligentsia and social elites.
    As a result, the victims included both Poles and Ukrainians, Jews and Belarusians — political and criminal prisoners without distinction of nationality.
▶ Comparison — Katyn Massacre (April-May 1940) On Stalin's and the Politburo's orders, the NKVD murdered approx. 22,000 Polish officers, police officers, civil servants, engineers and members of the intelligentsia — prisoners of war held in camps since September 1939. Executions by a shot to the back of the head were carried out at three locations: Katyn (a forest near Smolensk), Kharkiv and Kalinin (Tver). The victims were the elite of the Second Polish Republic — a deliberate target: the elimination of the future leadership of the Polish state.

The USSR denied responsibility for over half a century, blaming Germany. Only in 1990 did Gorbachev officially acknowledge the Politburo's guilt. Russia has never classified this crime as genocide.
1940-1941 UA controversies
▶ OUN split and ideological radicalisation

OUN split and ideological radicalisation. In February 1940 OUN split into two factions: OUN-M (Andriy Melnyk, moderate line) and OUN-B (Stepan Bandera, radical line). OUN-B under Bandera's leadership adopted a far more aggressive political doctrine, including a formal distinction between "friendly" and "hostile" population groups.

In OUN-B documents and rhetoric, the groups classified as hostile — treated as an obstacle to the creation of an independent Ukrainian state — included:

  • Poles — as historical oppressors and colonisers of Ukrainian lands
  • Russians — as representatives of Soviet imperialism
  • Jews — through adoption of antisemitic narratives
  • Ukrainians — as "traitors to the national cause" and opponents of OUN-B's line, e.g. persons helping Poles.

Quotes from OUN-B documents:

  • "Our rule will be terrible for our enemies. Terror against foreign enemies and our traitors."
  • "Muscovites, Poles, Jews and other enemies of Ukraine…"
1941 💀👥 crime against humanity
▶ OUN and the Germans — pogroms in Lviv

OUN and the Germans — pogroms. Yaroslav Stetsko proclaimed Ukrainian independence in Lviv on 30 June 1941; the Germans rejected it and arrested him. Part of OUN nonetheless participated in Jewish pogroms (Lviv 1941) and served in auxiliary police. The Ukrainian 14th Waffen-SS Division "Galicia" fought on the German side.

▶ Comparison — other "independences" under German patronage Slovakia (from March 1939): the government of Jozef Tiso, own army and administration — but German troops stationed on the territory, economy under Berlin's control, foreign policy dictated by the Reich. Slovakia deported its own Jews to extermination camps. Assessment: a puppet state, not sovereign.

Croatia — NDH (from April 1941): the Ustasha in power, own army and state structures — but territory divided between German and Italian spheres of influence. Assessment: a puppet state, not sovereign.
1941-1944 context
▶ German propaganda and infiltration

German propaganda. German propaganda emphasised:

  • Soviet crimes,
  • anti-communism,
  • antisemitic conspiracy theories,
  • promises of local autonomy (rarely fulfilled).

Abwehr and infiltration. The Abwehr, and later the Sicherheitsdienst (SD):

  • recruited local collaborators,
  • infiltrated resistance organisations,
  • employed double agents,
  • attempted to manipulate rival nationalist organisations.
1941-1942 UA controversies
▶ Roman Shukhevych — collaboration with the Germans

1941: Helped organise Ukrainian nationalist military formations linked to German intelligence, even before the German invasion of the USSR.

1941: Served as deputy commander of the Nachtigall battalion during the German advance into Ukraine.

1942: Joined the Schutzmannschaft and commanded a company of the 201st Battalion in Belarus. The 201st Schutzmannschaft Battalion is documented as having taken part in German anti-partisan operations in Belarus.

1941-1943 context
▶ Polesian Sich/UPA/UNRA — Taras Borovets

Polesian Sich/UPA/UNRA - Taras Borovets and OUN-B's seizure of the name. Taras Borovets in 1940 created an armed formation known as the Polesian Sich. In 1942 his units began using the name Ukrainian Insurgent Army (UPA).

In parallel, the Banderite faction of OUN (OUN-B) was building its own military structures. In 1942-1943 OUN-B seized the name UPA and gradually subordinated or destroyed rival Ukrainian armed formations, including a significant part of Borovets' units. The units that remained loyal to Borovets were by him restructured into the Ukrainian National Revolutionary Army (UNRA).

1941 - initial cooperation and fighting against the Soviets. After Germany's attack on the USSR, Borovets' units arose amid the collapse of Soviet administration. Initially the Germans tolerated the Polesian Sich's activities because they were directed against the Soviets. Over time the relationship deteriorated.

1942-1943 - shift toward fighting the German occupation. Borovets began partisan operations against the German occupation administration:

  • attacks on German posts and administration,
  • destruction or disruption of transport,
  • actions against the German economic apparatus (e.g. connected with resource exploitation and forced deliveries),
  • preventing the conscription of the Ukrainian population for forced labour in the Third Reich.
January-February 1943 context
▶ Change on the Eastern Front

Battle of Stalingrad (17 July 1942 - 2 February 1943). One of the key battles of the Second World War on the Eastern Front, ending in defeat for Germany and the surrender of the encircled 6th Army of the Wehrmacht. This defeat weakened the Wehrmacht's offensive potential and forced the Germans onto the defensive along large sections of the Eastern Front.

In January 1943, in parallel with the final phase of the Battle of Stalingrad, a Soviet offensive against German forces began, including Operation Iskra near Leningrad — part of the same broader shift on the Eastern Front.

The concentration of forces on holding the front meant Germany gradually lost control over parts of the occupied territories, creating power vacuums exploited by partisans, underground movements and other political forces.

February-March 1943 context
▶ Mass desertion of Ukrainians from the Ukrainian Auxiliary Police
  • OUN-B issued an order for policemen to transfer to UPA.
  • Desertion: approx. 4,000–6,000 policemen, taking weapons, ammunition and uniforms with them.
  • Result: UPA transformed from a small partisan force into a well-organised armed force.
  • Roman Shukhevych also joined UPA's units and, in November 1943, became its commander-in-chief.
💀 Volhynia Massacre (1943–1945)
February-March 1943 💀👥 crime against humanity

First mass attacks by UPA. Dozens of Polish villages in Volhynia burned, thousands of victims.

25 March 1943 context

Borovets' letter to OUN-B. Borovets protested against the Banderites' launch of the anti-Polish campaign. In his letter of 25 March 1943 he accused them of opening "yet another front" and that the victims were entire Polish families, women and children.

April 1943 context

Borovets' talks with OUN-B and the demand to "cleanse" Volhynia. Borovets attempted to reach an agreement with the Banderites to jointly fight the Germans and Soviets. OUN-B, however, wanted to subordinate his units to its own policy. According to historians, during negotiations on 9 April 1943 the Banderites reportedly demanded that he "cleanse" the territory of the Polish population. Borovets reportedly rejected this, arguing that an army should not punish entire nationalities for the actions of individual persons.

19 April 1943 - Krasny Sad 💀↩️ retaliatory atrocity (Germans/Police)

Massacre at Krasny Sad. Units of the Ordnungspolizei (German order police, which also included Polish collaborators) attacked the Ukrainian village of Krasny Sad. Approximately 104 Ukrainian civilians were killed, including women and children. The attack was a response to the ongoing UPA attacks on Polish villages in the area.

Early July - 6 July 1943 💀🪖 war crime

Zygmunt Rumel — emissary of peace. A Pole closely connected to Volhynia and an advocate of Polish-Ukrainian reconciliation. Sent by the AK as an emissary to negotiate with UPA in order to limit the conflict. Murdered by a UPA unit — torn apart by horses.

11 July 1943 - Bloody Sunday 💀👥 crime against humanity

Simultaneous attacks on approx. 167 localities during Sunday masses. Estimates: 5-8 thousand victims in one day.

August 1943 UA controversies

OUN-B operation against UNRA - attack on Borovets' headquarters. In August 1943 OUN-B units attacked Borovets' headquarters. His wife was captured and killed. This was part of a systematic elimination of independent Ukrainian armed formations by the Banderites.

Summer-Autumn 1943 💀👥 crime against humanity UA controversies

Systematic "cleansing" of Volhynia — UPA attacks successive villages and towns. Survivors flee to cities or form self-defence units.

UPA leaflets for the Polish population. Alongside the attacks, UPA distributed leaflets addressed to Poles living in Volhynia and Eastern Galicia. The content revolved around several recurring elements:

  • Demand to leave "Ukrainian lands" — Poles presented as intruders who must immediately leave territories described as ancient Ukrainian land.
  • Coloniser narrative — Poles described as colonisers and occupiers who had unlawfully seized Ukrainian land and lived at the expense of the Ukrainian people.
  • Warnings about the consequences of staying — leaflets explicitly stated that those who did not leave the designated areas within a set deadline would be treated as enemies and would face the consequences.

One of the preserved quotes from UPA leaflets reads: "Drive out the Poles from Ukrainian lands..."

Autumn 1943 - 1944 💀↩️ retaliatory atrocity

Polish reprisal operations. The Home Army (AK) and Peasant Battalions (BCh) attack Ukrainian villages suspected of collaboration with UPA. The operation was also directed at civilians.

February-March 1944 - Lviv 💀🪖 war crime

Murders in Lviv - Ukrainian Auxiliary Police. Officers of the Ukrainian Auxiliary Police (subordinate to the Germans) murdered (mainly members connected with UPA) Poles during document checks - taking victims' Kennkarten to assume new identities before the approaching Red Army.

AK retaliation: operation "Nieszpory" (Vespers) (9 and 12 March 1944) - 11 Ukrainian policemen killed; murders of Poles ceased.

March 1944 - Sahryń 💀↩️ retaliatory atrocity

A reprisal operation by the AK and BCh against the village of Sahryń (inhabited by Ukrainians). Victims: approx. 200-1,000 (historians' estimates vary significantly). A crime against civilians — unacceptable regardless of context.

▶ My Opinion These crimes cannot be attributed to the entire AK formation. This was not a total or primary activity of the organisation — it was carried out by local units acting outside the spirit and letter of orders. The AK had a formal rule forbidding attacks on civilians — which means Sahryń was a violation of the Underground State's own law, not its implementation. It is however controversial that the commanders responsible for these orders were never held to account — assuming it was known at higher levels at all.
1943-1944 - Eastern Galicia 💀👥 crime against humanity

UPA extends operations to Eastern Galicia — continuation of murders amid growing resistance from Polish self-defence units.

Balance 1943-1945 context

Approx. 50-120 thousand Poles killed by UPA, including a small Czech community (<2%).

Approx. 5-15 thousand Ukrainians killed in Polish reprisal operations.

Approx. 2-15 thousand Ukrainians killed by UPA (as "traitors", collaborators or political opponents of OUN-B).

The vast majority of victims on both sides were civilians.

Character of attacks and civilian casualties: In 1943-1945 UPA conducted coordinated attacks on Polish settlements in Volhynia and Eastern Galicia, often on days of great religious significance. On the Polish side there were reprisal operations that also struck Ukrainian civilians.

Methods of violence on both sides: These included raids on villages, arson of buildings, killings with cold steel and firearms, and executions. The scale and degree of organisation of UPA's actions were greater; however AK/BCh reprisal operations also led to the deaths of civilians, including women and children.

Extreme methods used by UPA: burning alive, drowning in wells and rivers, burying alive, impalement, nailing nails into heads, disembowelling pregnant women, dismemberment by horses, quartering - frequently forcing family members to watch the executions.

Survivors' means of escape: Those who survived fled and hid in various ways. Forests provided shelter for groups hiding for years, though exposing them to cold and lack of food. Churches and chapels sometimes served as shelter during sudden attacks, occasionally burning with the people inside. The best chance of survival lay in fleeing to towns and larger localities with a permanent presence of occupation forces (German or Soviet) — UPA generally avoided confrontation with regular units. Railway junctions and posts along railway lines formed relatively safe enclaves, as did Polish settlements with organised self-defence. The Polish population concentrated around such points, creating refugee settlements.

▶ Comparison — Ustasha and Serbs (1941-1945) The Croatian fascist movement Ustasha, after seizing power in the Independent State of Croatia (NDH) in 1941, carried out systematic extermination of Serbs, Jews and Roma — on the same territory, at the same time as UPA's crimes.

Scale: historians' estimates: 300-800 thousand Serbian victims (the discrepancy between Croatian and Serbian researchers is comparable to the Polish-Ukrainian dispute over Volhynia).

Methods: mass shootings, throat-cutting with special knives (srbosjek), burning alive in Orthodox churches, drowning in the Sava and Drina rivers, Jasenovac extermination camp (approx. 80-100 thousand victims).

Context: like UPA, the Ustasha were nationalists seeking an ethnically pure territory under the cover of German occupation. Some Croatians glorified them as national heroes.

Croatia 2000-2013 — official rejection of the Ustasha legacy:
1. In the constitution and official state narrative, continuity with the anti-fascist struggle of the Second World War was reinforced, not with the NDH. The NDH was unambiguously recognised as a collaborationist and criminal regime.
2. The state began consistently supporting the memory of Jasenovac camp victims — official memorial sites and annual commemorations under state institutional patronage.
3. In adapting to EU standards, the prohibition on promoting fascism and totalitarian symbols, including Ustasha ones, was strengthened (enforcement remains uneven).
4. In the EU accession process, Croatia was subject to pressure regarding rule of law, minority rights and cooperation with ICTY — which encouraged official distance from nationalist historical narratives.

The crimes in Volhynia were not an anomaly — they were part of a broader European phenomenon of nationalist extermination during the Second World War.
▶ Comparison — Lithuanians and Poles (1944) Mutual Polish-Lithuanian murders in the Vilnius region. In 1944 the Vilnius region became an arena of mutual murders between the Polish civilian population and AK partisans on one side, and Lithuanian auxiliary police and civilians on the other.

Glinciszki (20 June 1944) — the largest massacre of Poles. A Lithuanian auxiliary police unit (Ypatingasis būrys) shot 39 Polish civilians — men, women and children — in retaliation for earlier losses in clashes with the AK.

Dubingiai/Dubinki (23 June 1944) — AK response. Three days after Glinciszki, AK units carried out a retaliatory operation on the Lithuanian village of Dubingiai, killing approx. 27-65 Lithuanian civilians (Polish sources give the lower figure, Lithuanian sources the higher), including women and children. The victims were mostly unarmed residents — not the perpetrators of the earlier crime.

Civilian casualty balance:
  • Polish civilians killed by Lithuanian auxiliary police units (in collaboration with Germans): approx. 300-500 people in 1941-1944.
  • Lithuanian civilians killed by AK units: approx. 100-200 people.
There is a fundamental legal distinction: neither side classifies these events as genocide. The intent to systematically exterminate a nationality is absent — the murders were retaliatory and situational, not a plan for ethnic cleansing. This distinguishes them fundamentally from UPA's planned extermination on Volhynia.
1943-1945 ★ righteous UA
Ukrainian neighbours rescuing Poles.

One person could save 1-10 people; unfortunately most of those who helped have not been identified.

  • Hiding Poles in homes, barns, cellars or farmsteads.
  • Warning of planned attacks by UPA units or local groups participating in raids.
  • Helping escape to safer towns, cities or territories controlled by Germans or Soviets.
  • Providing food, clothing and shelter to those in hiding.
  • Guiding people through forests and endangered areas to relatively safe locations.
  • In individual cases, standing up in defence of Polish neighbours or refusing to participate in actions against them.

Many helpers risked their own lives. Historians have documented cases of Ukrainians killed by UPA members or local nationalists for helping Poles.

January 1944 - January 1945 context
▶ Operation Tempest

A general Polish military operation of the Home Army aimed at liberating Polish territories from German occupation just before the arrival of the Red Army — and demonstrating Polish sovereignty.

Objectives of the operation:

  • Armed action against the Germans as the Red Army approached, to show that Polish territories were being liberated by the legitimate Polish underground, not taken over by the Soviets.
  • Revealing the structures of the Polish Underground State and having the Home Army appear as the host of these territories before the advancing Red Army and the Western Allies.
  • Restoring Polish civilian administration in the liberated areas and preparing the return of the legitimate authorities of the Republic of Poland.
  • Inflicting losses on the Germans and speeding up their expulsion from Polish territory.

Important context: at the time the USSR formally acted as an allied army toward the Western Allies — the same status as the Polish Underground State and the Home Army.

The operation covered the Borderlands too:

  • Volhynia (1944). AK units fought the Germans as part of Operation Tempest. The 27th Volhynian Infantry Division AK was formed, conducting combat operations against German forces and preparing to establish Polish civilian administration.
  • Lviv (1944). AK carried out Operation Tempest in Lviv, fighting alongside Soviet forces against the Germans during the capture of the city.

After Soviet forces took control of the territories, the situation changed: AK units were disarmed, soldiers were arrested by the NKVD, and many were sent to prisons and labour camps.

▶ Example — Warsaw Uprising (August-October 1944) 63 days of fighting. Polish side: approx. 16,000 AK soldiers killed, approx. 150,000-200,000 civilian casualties. German side: approx. 8,000-10,000 killed, approx. 9,000 wounded. Destruction of Warsaw by the Germans after its fall.
1944-1946 context
▶ Forced population transfer

Forced population transfer. Approx. 750-800 thousand Poles resettled from Western Ukraine to Poland (so-called repatriation — in reality, expulsion). Approx. 500 thousand Ukrainians resettled from Poland to the USSR.

Note: the figure of 1.5 million usually covers a broader category — all Poles who left the Eastern Borderlands in 1944-1959 (i.e. including later departures, e.g. from territories annexed by the USSR after the war).

1944-1946 context
▶ Soviet operations against UPA

1944-1945: NKVD operations against UPA. Soviet security services conducted large-scale manhunts, arrests, executions and intelligence operations aimed at destroying UPA's networks in western Ukraine.

1945: Operation "Great Blockade". A major Soviet campaign using substantial military forces, aimed at encircling forests and areas suspected of harbouring UPA, inflicting heavy losses on the insurgents.

1945-1946: Soviet deportation campaigns. Soviet authorities deported families accused of supporting UPA to remote regions of the USSR, seeking to weaken the underground's social base.

NKVD false-flag operations. The NKVD sometimes:

  • disguised its own agents as nationalist fighters,
  • conducted operations imitating underground group activities,
  • used fake partisan units to track down resistance networks.

These tactics became especially widespread after 1944 in operations against Ukrainian insurgents.

1945-1947 PPR controversies
▶ UPA still active in Poland

UPA still active. After the end of the war, UPA continued partisan warfare against PPR and NKVD units in south-eastern Poland. These activities were used by the PPR authorities as justification for Operation Vistula.

17 April 1945 💀 atrocity
▶ Massacre at Wiazownica

Massacre at Wiazownica. UPA units attacked the Polish village of Wiazownica. Depending on the source, between approximately 90 and over 120 Polish civilians were killed, along with several soldiers of the People's Army of Poland (LWP).

Night of 17/18 April 1945 - Piskorowice 💀↩️ retaliatory atrocity
▶ Reprisal at Piskorowice

Reprisal at Piskorowice. On the night following the UPA attack on Wiazownica, conspiratorial NOW (National Military Organisation) units carried out a reprisal against the Ukrainian village of Piskorowice. Approximately 100-160 Ukrainians were killed, the majority of them civilians.

1945-1947 💀 PL atrocity
▶ Józef Kuraś "Ogień"

Józef Kuraś "Ogień" — a Podhale partisan commander, officially operating as an anti-communist cursed soldier.

Total number of victims of his units: approx. 200 people, of whom nearly half were civilian — mostly Poles and Slovaks living in the border region. Among documented crimes: murders of civilians suspected of collaborating with communists or deemed enemies on the basis of informants' reports, robberies and pacification of villages on both sides of the border.

Kuraś was killed in February 1947, surrounded in a manhunt by the PPR security apparatus.

▶ Opinion Kuraś is a figure who divides Polish historical memory. Fighting against communist occupation was justified and honourable — but the approx. 100 civilian victims, including Poles and Slovaks who cannot be called collaborators, constitute a crime that must be named plainly. Heroism toward the enemy does not erase crimes against the innocent. Treating him solely as a symbol of resistance without accounting for this aspect of his activities is the same error we attribute to the Ukrainian side in the case of UPA.
1947 PPR controversies
▶ Operation Vistula

Approx. 150,000 Ukrainians, Lemkos and Boyko were forcibly resettled from south-eastern Poland to the western territories — dispersed, with the aim of denationalisation. The operation was coordinated with the USSR and Czechoslovakia, which sealed the borders to prevent escape. Official justification: pacification of UPA's rear base. In practice: collective punishment of the entire civilian population.

▶ My Opinion It is worth remembering that the PPR is not regarded by most Poles as the legal successor to the Second Polish Republic. For the majority of Polish society — and for the Polish legal-state tradition — the only legitimate continuation was the government-in-exile in London. The PPR was a creation imposed by a foreign power, just like the communist governments in Czechoslovakia, Hungary or Romania.
