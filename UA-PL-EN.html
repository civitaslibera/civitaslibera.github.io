Polish-Ukrainian Conflict 1918–1947

Historical events have divided, do divide, and will divide nations — but what matters is being able to call a war criminal a war criminal.

Motivation and Disclaimers

The purpose of this document is to present the Polish-Ukrainian conflict as objectively as possible, taking into account the perspectives of both sides. This is not a propaganda narrative nor a settling of scores.

I am a Pole — and I am aware that this affects my perception of history. I am not a historian. I do, however, try to step beyond my own narrative: I know that Lviv was founded by the Rurikid dynasty, that Ruthenian magnates were an integral part of the nobility of the Polish-Lithuanian Commonwealth, and that Ukrainian historical identity in these lands is equal to Polish identity. I write this not to diminish Polish heritage, but to show that honest dialogue requires acknowledging facts on both sides.

This document is deliberately simplified — some facts, contexts, and events have been omitted so that it does not become an encyclopedia. The goal is not to exhaust the subject, but to present a general picture of those times in a clear and accessible way.

No reparation — however great — can restore the dignity of a victim killed in a bestial manner. This is not a matter of money or political gestures. The only thing we can do together is to preserve the memory of those ordinary people: their names, places, stories. So that they do not perish a second time — this time in oblivion.

Timeline 1918–1947

General Context 💀 Crime Controversies: Polish Ukrainian PRL
November 1918 – June 1919 context

Polish-Ukrainian War. Ukrainians proclaimed the Western Ukrainian People's Republic (WUPR) with Lviv as its capital. Poland won this war, taking control of Eastern Galicia and Volhynia.

1918–1919 💀 crime PL & UA

Pogroms against Jews during the Polish-Ukrainian War. Jews were victims of both sides of the conflict. Lviv pogrom (November 1918): after Polish forces captured Lviv, soldiers and civilians carried out a pogrom — estimates: 52–150 killed, hundreds wounded, widespread looting. The pretext was the accusation that Jews were neutral or sympathetic to Ukrainians. On the Ukrainian side, the units of Ataman Semosenko carried out pogroms in towns under their control (Proskuriv 1919 — approx. 1,500 victims). Jews were treated as outsiders by both sides — despite having lived in these lands for centuries.

▶ Comparison – pogroms during the Russian Civil War (1918–1921) The pogroms in the Borderlands were not an exception — they were part of a wave of anti-Jewish violence sweeping across the entire post-Soviet space:

White Army forces (Denikin, Wrangel): systematic pogroms in Ukraine in 1919, treated by some officers as part of the struggle against "Jewish Bolshevism." Estimates: 50–150 thousand Jewish deaths at the hands of the Whites.

Ukrainian atamans (Makhno, Semosenko and others): pogroms in hundreds of towns; total victims of Ukrainian forces estimated at 30–50 thousand.

Bolsheviks: officially opposed to antisemitism, but some units of the Red Army also carried out pogroms — on a significantly smaller scale.

The total number of Jews killed in all pogroms between 1918–1921 is estimated at 50–200 thousand — making this period one of the greatest waves of anti-Jewish violence before the Holocaust.
▶ My Opinion It is worth noting that both sides of the conflict — Polish and Ukrainian — formally condemned these crimes. The Second Polish Republic (II RP) conducted investigations into the Lviv pogrom and brought perpetrators before courts; verdicts were handed down. They were not spectacular, but given the chaos of the first months of the independent state, it was a decisive step. On the Ukrainian side, Petlura himself issued orders forbidding pogroms, and some of his officers actively tried to suppress them — though to no avail against unruly units. It is also significant that during 1921–1939 the II RP treated anti-Jewish actions by nationalists (beatings, boycotts, riots) as criminal offenses. This means the state as an institution did not discriminate against Jews at the legal level, though the same cannot be said of social attitudes and the actions of nationalist circles.
April 1920 context

Warsaw Agreement — the Piłsudski–Petlura alliance. Poland recognizes Symon Petlura's Ukrainian People's Republic (UPR) in exchange for Polish control over Eastern Galicia — the UPR formally renounces lands east of the Zbruch River. Polish-Ukrainian forces capture Kyiv, but the Red Army's counteroffensive reaches Warsaw (August 1920).

▶ Comparison – the same dynamic, 1939 British and French guarantees for Poland (1939): formal military commitments without real execution — Poland alone in the face of invasion. In both cases, the smaller partner in the alliance was sacrificed for a "higher goal."
▶ My Opinion The UPR's renunciation of territory was a forced decision, not a voluntary one. Petlura had no territory, no army, and no recognition from the great powers — the alliance with Poland was the only chance to return to Ukraine. The borders were meant to be a temporary matter.
March 1921 context

Treaty of Riga. Peace concluded without UPR participation, dividing Ukraine between Poland and Soviet Russia.

▶ Comparison – great powers deciding for others Yalta Conference (February 1945) — Roosevelt, Churchill, Stalin. Poland's borders and political system were determined without the participation of the Polish government-in-exile. Poland was shifted westward at the cost of its eastern territories.
▶ My Opinion As a Pole, I understand that feeling — we ourselves feel betrayed by the decisions of Yalta in 1945, where the great powers decided about Poland without Poles. Ukrainians in 1921 may have felt exactly the same: their fate was settled at a table where they had no seat.
1921–1939 PL controversy

Polish policy in the Borderlands. Systematic Polonization of the Borderlands through legal and demographic instruments:

  • Settlement (1920–1921). The Military Settlement Act (1920) granted land to veterans of the Polish-Bolshevik War: approx. 10,000 military settler families and approx. 5,000 civilian families — a total estimated at approx. 60,000 people settled in the Borderlands. The land came from the parceling of estates, often at the expense of Ukrainian peasants.
  • Language and administration (1924). Polish as the sole official language in administration and courts in the Borderlands — Ukrainians forced to use institutions in a foreign language.
  • Land reform (1925). The Land Reform Act favored Polish settlers in land distribution — Ukrainians as a minority had limited access despite constituting the majority of the rural population.
  • Church and religion. Favoritism toward the Roman Catholic Church (Polish) at the expense of the Greek Catholic and Orthodox Churches (Ukrainian) — including the seizure of churches and restrictions on parish activities.
  • Civil service. Ukrainians were practically excluded from higher positions in state administration and the military in the Borderlands.

This period was characterized by deep political instability in the II RP:

  • Assassination of Narutowicz (1922). Gabriel Narutowicz, the first President of Poland elected in part by the votes of national minorities (Ukrainians, Jews, Germans), was murdered after 5 days in office by a right-wing nationalist. This demonstrated the strength of right-wing nationalism and hostility toward a policy of minority inclusion.
  • Right-wing governments 1922–1926. Successive cabinets (Chjeno-Piast, center-right) pursued increasingly restrictive policies toward minorities — limiting autonomy, using military settlement in the Borderlands as a deliberate demographic tool. The culmination was the Grabski Act (1924) — a drastic reduction of Ukrainian-language schools and their replacement with "utraquist" (Polish-Ukrainian) schools, which in practice meant the Polonization of education.
  • May Coup (1926). Józef Piłsudski overthrew the democratically elected government and introduced an authoritarian system (sanacja). Paradoxically, his policy toward minorities was more pragmatic than that of the National Democratic right — however, he did not abolish discriminatory practices in the Borderlands.
▶ Comparison – the Bolsheviks toward Ukrainians (1920s) At the same time, on the eastern side of the border, the Bolsheviks were pursuing a policy of Ukrainization (korenizatsiya, 1923–1933) — actively supporting the Ukrainian language in schools, administration, culture, and media. Ukrainian became the official language of Soviet Ukraine. Ukrainian universities, theaters, and press were established.
▶ My Opinion Ukrainian discontent here is entirely understandable — what the Poles were doing in the Borderlands, the Poles themselves had experienced under the partitions as Germanization and Russification. We knew exactly how much it hurts, and we did the same thing. For a Ukrainian living under Polish Polonization, the Soviet model could have paradoxically seemed more attractive — at least until Stalin reversed that policy with the Holodomor and the liquidation of the Ukrainian intelligentsia. In hindsight, it is clear that Soviet tolerance was for show — a tool for drawing Ukrainians into structures of power, not a recognition of their rights.
1920–1929 UA controversy

The formation of the UMO and OUN. The Ukrainian Military Organization (UMO, 1920) — the first Ukrainian paramilitary organization; from it emerged the Organization of Ukrainian Nationalists (OUN, 1929). A wave of arson attacks on Polish farms and infrastructure sabotage.

Scale of activity:

  • Sabotage campaign of 1930 — approx. 2,000 acts of arson, destruction of telegraph lines, and railway sabotage in Eastern Galicia; the Pacification operation was a direct response to this campaign
  • Political assassinations 1921–1939 — over 60 confirmed assassinations and assassination attempts targeting Polish officials, police officers, and activists
  • Raids on post offices and banks (to finance operations) — a dozen or so documented actions
▶ Comparison – radicalization of nationalism on both sides During the same period, analogous extremist movements were emerging on the Polish side:

Camp of Great Poland (OWP, 1926) — Roman Dmowski, integral nationalism, antisemitism, the vision of an ethnically homogeneous Poland.

National Radical Camp (ONR, 1934) — a split from OWP, more radical, modeled on Italian and German fascism. Attacks on Jews, academic militias, the slogan of "Poland for Poles."

Context: the 1920s and 1930s were a period of radicalization across all of Europe — the NSDAP, Italian fascists, the Spanish Falange. Both OUN and ONR were children of the same era, not anomalies.
▶ My Opinion Resistance to subjugation is natural and justified. A nation deprived of other political instruments reaches for what it has left — and what we now call terrorism was at the time the only available form of resistance for the weaker side. It is worth noting, however, that OUN activities during this period were directed primarily at officials — civil servants, police officers, politicians — and not at the civilian population.
1930 PL controversy

Pacification of Eastern Małopolska (September–November 1930). The authorities' response to the OUN's sabotage campaign. Reprisals were administered collectively, without distinguishing the guilty.

  • Approx. 500–800 villages affected by the pacification operation
  • Approx. 500–800 homes and farm buildings destroyed or burned
  • Approx. 1,600–2,000 people arrested and interrogated, many beaten
  • Over 30 Prosvita reading rooms (Ukrainian cultural centers) vandalized or closed
  • Confiscation of property: horses, livestock, grain
  • Fatalities: several to a dozen or more (Polish and Ukrainian sources differ significantly)
▶ My Opinion The Pacification was a political move that revealed the weakness of the II RP more than its strength. Resorting to collective reprisals without legal basis, directed at entire villages instead of specific perpetrators, was an admission of inability to resolve the problem through legal means. The one thing that can be noted on the positive side — village headmen were given prior warning through an ultimatum, providing some advance notice. That, however, is far too little to call this a law-abiding operation.
1931–1934 UA controversy

OUN assassinations. A series of political murders targeting representatives of the Polish state:

  • 1931 — Tadeusz Hołówko (member of parliament, advocate of Polish-Ukrainian dialogue) — shot in Truskavets. Paradoxically, the OUN eliminated one of the few Poles genuinely open to compromise.
  • 1934 — Bronisław Pieracki (Minister of Internal Affairs) — shot in Warsaw. Perpetrator: Hryhoriy Matseiko on OUN-B orders. The assassination was the direct impetus for the creation of the Bereza Kartuska internment camp.
▶ My Opinion Political assassinations as a form of resistance against authority are a well-known phenomenon of that era and can be understood in that context. However, the killing of Hołówko made no sense to me. He was one of the few senior Polish politicians genuinely working toward a Polish-Ukrainian understanding — his "Prometheism" envisioned building alliances with nations subjugated by Russia, including Ukrainians. The OUN eliminated one of their potential allies on the Polish side. But perhaps I simply do not understand it.
1934–1939 PL controversy

Bereza Kartuska. The only internment camp of the II RP, established after the Pieracki assassination. Nationalists (OUN, ONR), communists, and opposition figures were held without trial. A total of approx. 3,000–3,500 prisoners; mortality: 13–30 people (~0.5%).

Instead of effective integration policies or law enforcement through the courts, the II RP resorted to detention without trial — a method that de facto admitted the legal system was insufficient. Compared to the Gulag, the scale is incomparable, but the principle is the same: isolation of political opponents outside the framework of law.
1932–1933 💀 crime (USSR)

Holodomor. A famine deliberately engineered by Soviet authorities, directed against the Ukrainian countryside. Victims: approx. 3.5–7 million Ukrainians.

17 September 1939 – 1941 context

First Soviet occupation. The USSR's entry into the Eastern Borderlands. Mass deportations of the Polish population into the depths of the USSR (approx. 320,000–400,000 people). Some Ukrainians welcomed the Soviets with relief — which entrenched the memory of mutual grievances on both sides. Just as the Polish elite was being systematically liquidated by the NKVD, so too was the Ukrainian elite — arrests, executions, and deportations struck Ukrainian activists, teachers, clergy, and intelligentsia.

▶ My Opinion The satisfaction of some Ukrainians at the Soviet entry likely stemmed from their experience with the Polish system — after years of Polonization and discrimination, any change could have seemed better. I suspect, however, that this illusion quickly dissolved — when the NKVD began arresting the same Ukrainians who had hoped for liberation, the reality of Soviet occupation became obvious. The Soviets did not bring freedom — they brought a different kind of subjugation.
1941 💀 UA crime

OUN and the Germans — pogroms. Bandera proclaimed Ukrainian independence in Lviv on 30 June 1941; the Germans rejected it and arrested him. Part of the OUN nonetheless participated in pogroms against Jews (Lviv 1941) and served in auxiliary police units. The Ukrainian 14th Waffen-SS Division "Galicia" fought on the German side.

▶ Comparison – other "independences" under German patronage Slovakia (from March 1939): the government of Jozef Tiso, its own army and administration — but German troops were stationed on its territory, the economy was under Berlin's control, and foreign policy was dictated by the Reich. Slovakia deported its own Jews to extermination camps. Assessment: a puppet state, not sovereign.

Croatia — NDH (from April 1941): the Ustasha in power, their own army and state structures — but the territory divided between German and Italian spheres of influence. Assessment: a puppet state, not sovereign.

February–March 1943 💀 UA crime

First mass attacks by the UPA. Dozens of Polish villages in Volhynia burned, thousands of victims.

11 July 1943 – Bloody Sunday 💀 UA crime

Simultaneous attacks on approx. 167 localities during Sunday Masses. Estimates: 5,000–8,000 victims in a single day.

▶ My Opinion I consider the UPA's actions to have been systematic and planned, not spontaneous. Several facts support this: the attacks encompassed many villages simultaneously across a vast area — practically the entire territory under UPA influence. They lasted for months. If they had resulted solely from accumulated hatred and emotion, why did they not erupt earlier — under Polish rule, when Ukrainian grievances were fresh? The operation began at the moment when the II RP no longer existed, the Germans and Soviets were bleeding each other dry, and Volhynia was a land without a real sovereign. This was a political and military calculation, not an eruption of rage. There are no unambiguous documents ordering extermination — which the Ukrainian side often raises. But actions speak louder than documents. A partisan movement by definition does not leave written orders for the extermination of civilians — it is not a regular army with a chancellery. Viewed through the lens of partisan warfare: coordinating hundreds of attacks in a single day, across a vast territory, using similar methods, requires command and planning. The absence of a document does not mean the absence of a decision.
Summer–Autumn 1943 💀 UA crime

Systematic "cleansing" of Volhynia — UPA attacks successive villages and towns. Survivors flee to cities or form self-defense units.

Autumn 1943 – 1944 💀 PL crime

Polish retaliatory actions. The Home Army and Peasant Battalions attack Ukrainian villages suspected of collaboration with the UPA. The scale is incomparable to UPA operations, but the attacks were also directed at civilians.

March 1944 – Sahryń 💀 PL crime

A retaliatory operation by the AK and BCh against the village of Sahryń (inhabited by Ukrainians). Victims: approx. 200–1,000 (historians' estimates vary widely). A crime against civilians — unacceptable regardless of context.

▶ My Opinion These crimes cannot be attributed to the AK as a whole formation. This was not a totalizing activity nor the main purpose of the organization — they were carried out by local units, acting outside the spirit and letter of orders. The AK had a formal provision prohibiting attacks on civilians — which means Sahryń was a violation of Underground State law, not its execution. What is controversial, however, is that the commanders responsible for these orders were not held accountable — assuming it was even known at higher levels.
1943–1944 – Eastern Galicia 💀 UA crime

The UPA extends its operations to Eastern Galicia — continuation of killings amid growing resistance from Polish self-defense units.

▶ My Opinion Combat against armed combatants cost the UPA approx. 15,000 losses on the Soviet side, approx. 5,000 on the Polish side, and fewer than 1,000 on the German side. This is food for thought — attacks on civilians clearly carried far greater weight for UPA commanders than actual fighting against the occupier.
Balance Sheet 1943–1945 context

Approx. 60,000–100,000 Poles killed by the UPA. Approx. 10,000–15,000 Ukrainians killed in Polish retaliatory actions. The vast majority of victims on both sides were civilians.

Context of the attacks: The UPA deliberately chose Sundays and religious holidays.

UPA methods: burning alive, drowning in wells and rivers, burying alive, impalement, driving nails into heads, slitting the stomachs of pregnant women, tearing apart with horses, axes, saws, pitchforks.

AK/BCh methods (retaliatory actions): burning Ukrainian villages, executions, beatings. Although the scale and systematic nature were incomparable to UPA operations, the attacks also struck the civilian population, including women and children.

▶ Comparison – Ustasha and Serbs (1941–1945) The Croatian fascist Ustasha movement, after seizing power in the Independent State of Croatia (NDH) in 1941, carried out a systematic extermination of Serbs, Jews, and Roma — on the same territory, at the same time as the UPA's crimes.

Scale: historians' estimates: 300,000–800,000 Serbian victims (the discrepancies between Croatian and Serbian researchers are comparable to the Polish-Ukrainian dispute over Volhynia).

Methods: mass shootings, throat-cutting with special knives (srbosjek), burning alive in Orthodox churches, drowning in the Sava and Drina rivers, the Jasenovac extermination camp (approx. 80,000–100,000 victims).

Context: like the UPA, the Ustasha were nationalists seeking an ethnically pure territory under the cover of German occupation. Some Croatians glorified them as national heroes.

Croatia 2000–2013 — official distancing from the Ustasha legacy:
1. In the constitution and official state narrative, continuity was anchored in the anti-fascist struggle of World War II, not in the NDH. The NDH was unequivocally recognized as a collaborationist and criminal regime.
2. The state began consistently supporting the memory of victims of the Jasenovac camp — official memorial sites and anniversary commemorations under state institutional patronage.
3. In alignment with EU standards, the prohibition on promoting fascism and totalitarian symbols, including Ustasha ones, was strengthened (enforcement remains uneven).
4. In the EU accession process, Croatia was subject to pressure regarding the rule of law, minority rights, and cooperation with the ICTY — which favored an official distancing from nationalist historical narratives.

The crimes in Volhynia were not an anomaly — they were part of a broader European phenomenon of nationalist extermination during World War II.
1944–1946 context

Forced population exchange. Approx. 1.5 million Poles were resettled from the Borderlands to Poland (so-called repatriation — in reality, expulsion). Approx. 500,000 Ukrainians were resettled from Poland to the USSR.

1945–1947 – UPA vs. PRL PRL controversy

UPA still active. After the end of the war, the UPA continued partisan fighting against PRL and NKVD units in south-eastern Poland. These activities were used by the PRL authorities as justification for Operation Vistula.

1947 – Operation Vistula PRL controversy

Approx. 150,000 Ukrainians, Lemkos, and Boyks were forcibly resettled from south-eastern Poland to the western territories — scattered, with the aim of denationalization. Official justification: pacification of the UPA's rear base. In practice: collective punishment of the entire civilian population.

▶ My Opinion It is worth remembering that the PRL is not recognized by the majority of Poles as the legal successor to the II RP. For most of Polish society — and for Poland's legal-state tradition — the only legitimate continuation was the government-in-exile in London. The PRL was an entity imposed by a foreign power, just like the communist governments in Czechoslovakia, Hungary, and Romania.

My Opinion

On the Second Polish Republic

The II RP was the Poland that was possible — not ideal. A state rebuilt after 123 years of partition, stitched together from three different legal and administrative systems, surrounded from the outset by hostile neighbors. It discriminated against minorities, turned authoritarian after 1926, and never escaped poverty. And yet — against the backdrop of the totalitarianisms that destroyed it — it remained a space where an independent press, courts, and political parties functioned. This is not a defense of its mistakes. It is the context without which any assessment is dishonest.

On Ukrainians Under Two Occupations

Ukrainians found themselves in a tragic position — between repressive Polish administration and Soviet genocide. Aspirations for their own state were entirely justified. Seeking collaboration with the Germans was a political mistake and a moral catastrophe.

The UPA's actions were not a spontaneous retaliation for years of oppression — they were a planned extermination operation directed against civilians. The scale and methods cannot be justified by any context.

On Heroes and War Criminals

One can recognize one's own nation's heroes without falsifying history. Example: the Lviv Eaglets and WUPR soldiers fought for the same city on opposite sides — this diminishes the heroism of neither. All the more so, there is no need to seek heroes among those who committed crimes against civilians.

One can argue that Volhynia was retaliation for years of Polish occupation. One can argue that AK operations were retaliation for the murders of Poles. However, in both cases the same question arises: what did that child do? If children were punished with death — no one was looking at guilt. People were dehumanized and murdered to vent bestial emotions that someone called justice.

What Now?

Rebuilding relations after such experiences is one of the most difficult tasks two nations can face. It is not about forgetting — that would be impossible and dishonest toward the victims. It is about something harder: the ability to look at another person through a wall of memories, grievances, and mutual wrongs. This requires work, time, sacrifice, and — above all — the will on both sides. There are no shortcuts.

The crimes have been committed. We cannot undo them; we should not bid against each other over them. The most important thing is to commemorate the ordinary people — killed solely because they were in that place and spoke a different language. Do we really want hatred to be what we leave behind?

Questions to Start With – Ukrainian Side

  • Does Ukrainian national identity really need the UPA as a symbol? Ivan Mazepa, Taras Shevchenko, Mykhailo Hrushevsky — they fought for Ukraine without murdering their neighbors' children. Western Europe has found the line between the honor of resistance and the glorification of war criminals — can Ukraine do the same?
  • Could shared memorial sites and dignified exhumations of Volhynia's victims be the first concrete step — not a political gesture, but a human minimum toward families who to this day do not know where their loved ones lie?
  • Is the Ukrainian historical narrative capable of acknowledging that the victims of Volhynia were not "Polish oppressors" — but ordinary people, peasants, children, priests, who had nothing to do with the decisions of Warsaw politicians?
  • Does Ukraine need the UPA to have something to be proud of? Kyivan Rus, the Cossack Hetmanate, the Holodomor, the struggle for independence in 1918–1920 — Ukrainian history is rich and dramatic. Why should the symbol of strength and resistance be precisely the one who murdered neighbors' children?

Questions to Start With – Polish Side

  • Can we speak honestly about the II RP — acknowledging that it discriminated against minorities and was authoritarian — without feeling that we are betraying the memory of the generation that built it?
  • Can the retaliatory actions of the AK and BCh against Ukrainian civilians be called by their proper name — without the feeling that we are relativizing the crimes of Volhynia?
  • Are we able to acknowledge that some of those recognized as great fathers of the nation made decisions that were at least controversial? Piłsudski overthrew a democratically elected government and ruled autocratically. Dmowski built his vision of Poland on antisemitism and ethnic exclusivity. The pacification policies in the Borderlands were carried out by people we revere today. Acknowledging this is not betrayal — it is maturity.
  • Are we able to state plainly that slogans such as "Poland for Poles" or "Polish Vilnius" are nationalist slogans — and that they should be condemned, just as we condemn the nationalism of other nations? Polish identity does not need to define itself through exclusion.

The list of questions for both sides is incomplete — and intentionally so. I believe that a person rooted in Ukrainian education and the Ukrainian community would raise far more questions directed at their own side, just as I could expand this list on our Polish side. What I have recorded here is a possible point of entry — not a conclusion.
